Before & After

How humanity decided to number years — the story of BC, CE, and the missing year zero.

01 — The concept

Every calendar needs an anchor

All year-numbering systems need a fixed reference point — a moment declared "year one" from which all other years are counted. The BC/CE system chose the estimated birth year of Jesus of Nazareth as that anchor, calculated in 525 CE by a monk named Dionysius Exiguus.

From that anchor, years radiate outward in both directions. Going forward in time, years count upwards (1 CE, 2 CE, 3 CE…). Going backward in time, years also count upwards — which is where the confusion begins.

In BC, larger numbers = older. 500 BC is far more ancient than 10 BC.

02 — Terminology

Four terms, two meanings

The system has two naming conventions — one religious, one secular. The years themselves are identical; only the framing differs. 44 BC and 44 BCE are the exact same year.

BC
Before Christ
Years before the anchor. Counts backwards — a larger number means an older date. The Roman Empire fell in 476 CE; Julius Caesar was assassinated in 44 BC.
= BCE in secular use
AD
Anno Domini — "In the year of the Lord"
Years after the anchor. Counts forwards normally. Uniquely, AD traditionally precedes the year: "AD 476" — because it is a Latin phrase, not a suffix.
= CE in secular use
BCE
Before Common Era
The secular equivalent of BC. Adopted by academia, science, and interfaith contexts to remove religious framing while keeping identical year numbers.
= BC in traditional use
CE
Common Era
The secular equivalent of AD. Now standard in archaeology, history, and most scientific literature. Follows the year as a suffix, unlike AD.
= AD in traditional use

03 — The quirk

There is no Year Zero

When Dionysius designed the calendar in 525 CE, he used Roman numerals — and Romans had no concept of zero. The calendar skips directly from 1 BC to 1 CE, a gap of just one year, not two.

Astronomers fix this with Year 0
To make arithmetic clean, astronomers use their own system where Year 0 exists and equals 1 BC. In astronomical notation: 1 BC = 0, 2 BC = −1, 3 BC = −2, and so on. This means calculating the span between two dates is a simple subtraction — no special casing needed for the BC/CE boundary.

05 — Global perspective

Other cultures, other anchors

The BC/CE system is not universal. It became a global standard largely through trade and colonialism. Many civilisations use parallel calendars with entirely different anchor points — some still in active official or ceremonial use today.

Islamic (Hijri)
1446 AH
Year 1 = 622 CE. The anchor is Muhammad's migration from Mecca to Medina. Uses a lunar calendar of 354–355 days, so it drifts ~11 days per Gregorian year.
Hebrew
5785 AM
Counts from a calculated date of creation. Anno Mundi — "year of the world." A lunisolar calendar that adds a leap month 7 times in 19 years.
Indian (Saka Era)
1946 SE
Begins 78 CE. India's official national calendar alongside the Gregorian system. Used for gazette publications and government communications.
Japanese (Reiwa)
Reiwa 7
Resets with each emperor's reign. The current era, Reiwa, began May 1, 2019 CE. Japan uses both the imperial era system and Gregorian years in parallel.
Chinese
Year 4722
References the legendary Yellow Emperor (~2697 BC). A complex lunisolar system. The Chinese New Year falls on a different Gregorian date each year.
Ethiopian
2017 EE
About 7–8 years behind the Gregorian calendar. Ethiopia celebrated the year 2000 in 2007 CE. Uses the ancient Alexandrian calendar with 13 months.
Time is a human invention.

The BC/CE system is one of many ways humanity has chosen to measure its place in history.
